# Daron Acemoglu & James Robinson — *Why Nations Fail* (2012)
### Institutions, Power, and the Origins of Prosperity

---

## The Central Question

Why are some nations wealthy and others poor?

Common explanations:
- Geography (tropical climates, natural resources)
- Culture (work ethic, values, religion)
- Ignorance (leaders don't know the right policies)

**Acemoglu & Robinson's claim:** These explanations are wrong — or at best, incomplete. The real answer is: **institutions**.

---

## What Are Institutions?

Not just organisations or buildings. Institutions are the **rules of the game:**
- Who holds political power, and how
- Who can participate in the economy
- Whether property rights are enforced
- Whether contracts are honoured
- Whether innovation is rewarded or punished

Institutions shape incentives — and incentives shape behaviour.

---

## Two Types of Economic Institution

**Inclusive Economic Institutions:**
- Secure property rights for broad populations
- Allow free entry into markets
- Reward innovation and effort
- Enable creative destruction

**Extractive Economic Institutions:**
- Concentrate wealth in the hands of elites
- Block competition to protect incumbent power
- Discourage investment and innovation
- Exploit the majority for the benefit of the few

---

## Two Types of Political Institution

**Inclusive Political Institutions:**
- Distribute power broadly
- Constrain arbitrary use of power
- Enforce rule of law
- Allow political participation and accountability

**Extractive Political Institutions:**
- Concentrate power in a narrow elite
- Face no meaningful checks or accountability
- Use the state to enrich themselves
- Suppress opposition and competition

---

## The Virtuous and Vicious Circles

Inclusive institutions tend to reinforce each other:

> Broad political power → inclusive economic rules → prosperity → stake in the system → stable inclusive politics

Extractive institutions also reinforce each other:

> Concentrated power → rigged economy → wealth flows to elite → elite uses wealth to entrench political control

This is why poverty and wealth both tend to persist.

---

## Creative Destruction

Borrowed from economist Joseph Schumpeter. Economic growth requires innovation — but innovation disrupts existing industries and displaces existing elites.

Inclusive institutions tolerate this disruption. Extractive elites fear it — and use political power to block it.

**Example:** The Austro-Hungarian Empire resisted railways to protect landowners and prevent the movement of labour.

---

## Critical Junctures

History is not destiny — but it matters. A **critical juncture** is a moment of major disruption:
- The Black Death
- Colonial encounter
- Revolution or war
- Technological shock

At these moments, the balance of power shifts and institutions can change. Whether they become more inclusive or more extractive depends on who holds power at that moment.

---

## The Role of Colonialism

Acemoglu & Robinson use colonialism as a natural experiment.

Where European settlers could survive and settle (temperate climates), they built inclusive institutions — property rights, rule of law — which persisted and generated long-run prosperity.

Where disease made settlement impossible (tropical colonies), Europeans set up extractive institutions to exploit resources and labour — which also persisted, and generated long-run poverty.

Same coloniser, different institutions, different outcomes. This is the **reversal of fortune**.

---

## Can Growth Happen Under Extractive Institutions?

Yes — but not sustainably. The Soviet Union achieved rapid industrialisation through extractive means, but eventually stagnated.

Why? Without inclusive institutions:
- Innovation is suppressed
- Incentives weaken
- No creative destruction
- No political accountability to correct mistakes

**Growth without inclusive institutions hits a ceiling.**

---

## Comparative Table

| | Inclusive | Extractive |
|--|-----------|------------|
| **Political power** | Distributed broadly | Concentrated in elite |
| **Economic access** | Open to all | Restricted by elite |
| **Innovation** | Encouraged | Feared and blocked |
| **Long-run outcome** | Sustained prosperity | Stagnation or collapse |

---

## Key Criticisms

- **Too institution-centric:** Does it underweight geography, culture, or leadership?
- **Circular reasoning:** How do we identify inclusive institutions independently of their outcomes?
- **Agency problem:** If extractive elites block change, how does change ever happen?
- **Singapore problem:** High growth + restricted political competition seems to complicate the clean thesis.

---

## The Core Takeaway

Poverty is not natural or inevitable. It is produced — by institutions that concentrate power and exclude people. Prosperity is also produced — by institutions that distribute power and broaden participation.

**The question for any society is: who designs the rules, and in whose interest?**
