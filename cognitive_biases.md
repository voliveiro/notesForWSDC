# Thinking Against Ourselves
### Ten Cognitive Biases That Matter for Policy and Business

---

## Introduction: The Rational Actor Problem

Liberal political theory and classical economics share a foundational assumption: human beings are rational actors — they gather information, weigh options, and choose what is best for them.

This assumption underlies free market economics, democratic theory, contract law, and public policy design.

Decades of behavioural research have systematically dismantled it. We are not irrational — but we are **predictably irrational.**

---

## Bias 1: Confirmation Bias

The tendency to seek out, interpret, and remember information that confirms what we already believe.

**Policy example:** Governments commission research expecting it to support their preferred policy — and interpret ambiguous findings as confirmation.

**Business example:** Executives surround themselves with people who agree with their strategy.

---

## Bias 2: Availability Heuristic

We judge the likelihood of events by how easily examples come to mind. Vivid, recent, or emotionally powerful events feel more probable than they are.

**Policy example:** After a terrorist attack, governments massively over-invest in counter-terrorism relative to statistically larger risks like road deaths or diabetes.

**Business example:** After a high-profile startup failure, investors avoid an entire sector regardless of underlying fundamentals.

The media amplifies availability bias — what gets covered feels like what matters most.

---

## Bias 3: Status Quo Bias

We prefer the current state of affairs, even when change would be beneficial. Losses feel larger than equivalent gains — so inaction feels safer than action.

**Policy example:** Pension reform, healthcare restructuring, and tax reform are perpetually deferred because the costs of change feel more real than the benefits.

**Business example:** Incumbent companies fail to cannibalise their own products — creating openings for disruptors.

---

## Bias 4: In-Group Bias

We favour members of our own group — and are systematically less fair to out-group members, often without awareness.

**Policy example:** Research consistently shows that CVs with "foreign-sounding" names receive fewer callbacks, even from evaluators who believe they are being objective.

**Business example:** Hiring, promotion, and investment decisions systematically favour people who look like the decision-maker.

---

## Bias 5: Present Bias

We overweight immediate costs and benefits relative to future ones — even when we know the future matters more.

**Policy example:** Climate change policy is chronically under-resourced because the costs are now and the benefits are later.

**Business example:** Quarterly earnings pressure leads companies to under-invest in long-term R&D.

This is one reason institutions need to be designed to protect future generations — we cannot rely on individual motivation alone.

---

## Bias 6: Anchoring

We rely too heavily on the first piece of information we receive when making decisions. Subsequent information is interpreted relative to the anchor — even when the anchor is arbitrary.

**Policy example:** Opening positions in international negotiations set anchors that shape final agreements far more than the underlying merits.

**Business example:** The first salary figure mentioned in a negotiation heavily influences the final outcome — regardless of who says it.

**Implication:** whoever frames the debate first has a structural advantage.

---

## Bias 7: Overconfidence Bias

We systematically overestimate the accuracy of our own judgments and the likelihood of our preferred outcomes.

**Policy example:** Infrastructure projects almost universally run over time and over budget — planners consistently underestimate complexity and cost.

**Business example:** The vast majority of startups fail, yet founders consistently rate their own chances of success as well above average.

---

## Bias 8: Framing Effect

Identical information presented differently produces systematically different decisions.

**Policy example:** "90% survival rate" and "10% mortality rate" describe the same thing — but the first produces more support for a medical procedure than the second.

**Business example:** "£5 surcharge for cash" feels worse than "£5 discount for card" — even though they are economically identical.

**Implication:** policy communication is never neutral. How a choice is presented is itself a choice.

---

## Bias 9: Sunk Cost Fallacy

We continue investing in a failing course of action because of what we have already spent — rather than evaluating future costs and benefits.

**Policy example:** Governments continue funding failing infrastructure projects or military interventions because admitting failure is politically costly.

**Business example:** Companies persist with failing product lines rather than write off past investment.

The rational response — ignore sunk costs, evaluate only future costs and benefits — is psychologically very difficult.

---

## Bias 10: Fundamental Attribution Error

We attribute other people's behaviour to their character, while attributing our own behaviour to circumstances.

**Policy example:** Poverty is attributed to laziness or poor choices rather than structural conditions — which shapes welfare policy design.

**Business example:** A competitor's success is attributed to luck; our own success is attributed to skill.

---

## What This Means for Policy Design

If citizens are not rational actors, policy cannot simply provide information and expect rational response.

Enter **behavioural economics** and **nudge theory** (Thaler & Sunstein): design choices to make the rational option the default, use framing deliberately and transparently, build in commitment devices to overcome present bias, and reduce the cognitive load of good decisions.

But nudging raises its own questions — **who decides what the "right" choice is?**

---

## The Liberal Dilemma

Liberal theory assumes rational autonomous agents. Cognitive bias research shows we are not. This creates a dilemma:

- If people are not rational, paternalistic intervention may be justified
- But who designs the interventions, and in whose interest?
- Can we trust governments or corporations to nudge us toward genuinely good outcomes?

The same institutions that are supposed to serve us can capture behavioural insights to serve themselves.

---

## The Core Takeaway

Cognitive biases are not a sign of stupidity — they are features of human psychology, present in everyone.

The question is not how to eliminate them — that is probably impossible. The question is: **how do we design institutions, policies, and deliberative processes that account for them?**

A politics that assumes rational actors will consistently disappoint. A politics that takes human psychology seriously might not.
