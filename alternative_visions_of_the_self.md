# Alternative Visions of the Self
### How Religious and Philosophical Traditions Challenge Liberal Individualism

---

## The Liberal Self

Liberalism's foundational assumption: the self is the basic unit of political life. This self is bounded and separate from others, prior to its roles and relationships, self-creating and self-defining, the author of its own values and ends, and best fulfilled through maximising autonomy and choice.

This assumption is so deeply embedded in modern life that it feels like common sense. But across the world's great religious and philosophical traditions, it is the exception — not the rule.

---

## Buddhism: The Self Is an Illusion

The Buddha's teaching of *anatman* (no-self): there is no fixed, permanent self. What we call "the self" is actually five *skandhas* — aggregates constantly in flux: form, sensation, perception, mental formations, and consciousness. No stable core exists beneath them.

**Key implications:**
- Clinging to the self is the root of *dukkha* (suffering)
- The liberal project of self-maximisation is the fundamental spiritual error
- Liberation (*nirvana*) comes not from expressing the self but from seeing through it
- Compassion (*karuna*) flows naturally once the illusion of a separate self dissolves — the boundary between self and other becomes permeable

**The challenge to liberalism:** Buddhism doesn't say the self should be subordinated to community. It says the self, as liberals conceive it, doesn't exist.

---

## Christianity: The Self Must Die to Be Reborn

Christianity doesn't deny the self exists — but insists it cannot save itself and must be radically transformed.

*Kenosis* — self-emptying. Paul's letter to the Philippians: Christ "emptied himself," taking the form of a servant. The spiritual life involves imitating this self-emptying. The self is not maximised but poured out.

*Death and resurrection* — "Unless a grain of wheat falls into the earth and dies, it remains alone; but if it dies, it bears much fruit" (John 12:24). The liberal self wants to remain intact and maximise its options. The Christian self must undergo a kind of death — of ego, of self-will, of the project of self-sufficiency.

*Augustine's restless heart* — "Our heart is restless until it rests in Thee." The self is not self-sufficient. It is constituted by a longing for something beyond itself. Liberal anthropology says the self is complete and chooses its ends. Augustine says the self is *oriented* — it has a direction built into it, and is disordered when it turns back on itself (*incurvatus in se* — curved in on itself).

*The body of Christ* — Paul's ecclesiology in 1 Corinthians 12. The community of believers is not a collection of autonomous individuals who have chosen to associate. It is a single body in which each member has a function, and suffering and joy are shared. "If one member suffers, all suffer together."

*Iris Murdoch and Simone Weil* — both argue that the moral life requires *unselfing* — quieting the ego so that genuine attention to reality and to others becomes possible. Weil: the self must be *decreated* — unmade — so that love can flow through it unimpeded.

**The challenge to liberalism:** The self is real but radically incomplete. It is not the author of its own ends — it is oriented toward something beyond itself, and flourishes only through self-giving, not self-maximisation.

---

## Islam: Surrender as the Highest Freedom

*Islam* literally means submission or surrender. The highest human act is not self-assertion but *tawakkul* — trust in and surrender to God.

The liberal self chooses its own ends. The Muslim self discovers its ends in submission to divine will — and finds in that surrender not oppression but *true* freedom, because the self is now aligned with its deepest nature (*fitra*).

The *umma* — the community of believers — is not a voluntary association of autonomous individuals. It is a prior community that constitutes identity. Individual rights exist within and are subordinated to communal obligations.

**The challenge to liberalism:** Freedom is not the absence of constraint — it is alignment with one's true nature. Submission is not the opposite of flourishing — it is its condition.

---

## Judaism: The Commanded Self

The Jewish tradition offers yet another model: the *commanded self*. Rather than the liberal self who chooses its obligations, the Jewish self is already obligated — by covenant, by Torah, by the inheritance of a people's history.

Emmanuel Levinas: before I have chosen anything, the face of the other makes an infinite claim on me. **Responsibility precedes freedom.** The liberal order gets it exactly backwards — it starts with freedom and then asks what obligations I choose to take on.

Rabbi Jonathan Sacks: the liberal emphasis on rights produces a culture of entitlement without obligation. What holds societies together is not rights but *covenantal* relationships — bonds of mutual obligation that are not chosen but inherited and renewed.

**The challenge to liberalism:** Obligation is not a constraint on the self — it is constitutive of it. We are not free agents who then choose our responsibilities. We are already responsible before we choose anything.

---

## Hinduism: The Self Is Brahman

The *Advaita Vedanta* tradition (Shankara): the individual self (*atman*) is ultimately identical with the universal self (*Brahman*). The separate, bounded individual self is *maya* — illusion.

The goal of spiritual life is not self-expression or self-maximisation but *moksha* — liberation from the illusion of separateness, and realisation of the unity underlying all apparent diversity.

**The challenge to liberalism:** The bounded individual self is not the fundamental unit of reality — it is a misperception. The deepest truth is not individuation but unity.

## Confucianism: The Relational Self

Confucius begins not with the individual but with the relationship. His doctrine of **zhengming** — the rectification of names — is his starting point for social and political order.

When asked what he would do first if given power, Confucius said: rectify names. Why? Because if names are not correct, speech does not accord with truth; if speech does not accord with truth, affairs cannot be accomplished; if affairs cannot be accomplished, ritual and music cannot flourish; if ritual and music do not flourish, social order collapses.

**What Confucius means by "names"**

Not just labels — but the roles and relationships that constitute social life. The Five Relationships (*wu lun*): father and son, ruler and minister, husband and wife, elder and younger brother, friend and friend.

These are not optional associations that autonomous individuals choose to enter. They are the fabric of what it means to be human. You are not a person who *has* a father — you are, in part, constituted by *being a son.*

**Rectification means: be what you are**

If you are a father, fulfil the obligations fatherhood requires. If you are a ruler, rule justly. Holding a role without fulfilling its obligations corrupts the social fabric. Society unravels when roles become empty titles.

**The cultivation dimension**

Confucius doesn't just say: accept your role. He says: *cultivate* the virtues your role requires. Central to this is *ren* — benevolence or humaneness — not a private feeling but a quality expressed through relationships. You become more fully human by becoming more fully present to your relational obligations.

**The political implication**

Good government is not about protecting individual rights or maximising aggregate welfare. It is about creating conditions in which people can fulfil their relational roles well — and in which rulers model the virtues they expect of others.

**The challenge to liberalism:** The self is not prior to its relationships — it is constituted by them. Identity is not chosen from the inside out but cultivated from the outside in, through faithful inhabiting of inherited roles and obligations.

## Comparative Table

| Tradition | Nature of the Self | Path to Flourishing | Challenge to Liberalism |
|--|--|--|--|
| **Buddhism** | Illusion — no fixed self | Dissolve the self | The autonomous self doesn't exist |
| **Christianity** | Real but incomplete, oriented beyond itself | Self-emptying and transformation | The self flourishes through giving, not maximising |
| **Islam** | Real but finds true freedom in surrender | Submission to divine will | Freedom is alignment with nature, not absence of constraint |
| **Judaism** | Real but already obligated | Fulfil inherited covenantal obligations | Responsibility precedes freedom |
| **Hinduism** | Individual self is illusion — atman is Brahman | Realise unity with universal self | The bounded self is a misperception |
| **Confucianism** | Constituted by relationships and roles | Cultivate virtue within inherited relationships | The relationship, not the individual, is the basic unit |

---

## What These Traditions Share

Despite profound differences, all six traditions agree on this:
- The liberal self — bounded, autonomous, self-creating, self-maximising — is a distortion or illusion
- The self is constituted by relationships, obligations, or longings it did not choose
- Genuine flourishing involves self-transcendence rather than self-maximisation
- Community, tradition, and inherited obligation are not constraints on the self — they are constitutive of it

---

## The Core Takeaway

Across radically different cultures and centuries, the autonomous liberal self is the outlier — not the universal human default that liberalism presents it as.

If almost every major tradition of human wisdom challenges this conception of the self, the burden of proof lies with liberalism — not with its critics.

**The question for political thinking is not: how do we protect the autonomous self?**
**It is: what kind of self do we want to cultivate — and within what relationships, obligations, and traditions?**
