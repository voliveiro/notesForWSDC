# Fredrik deBoer — *How Elites Ate the Social Justice Movement* (2023)
### Why the Left Keeps Losing

---

## The Central Provocation

In 2020, the United States experienced its most intense wave of social protest since the 1960s. Tens of thousands in the streets. Major corporations pledging solidarity. Institutions scrambling to respond.

Then: almost nothing changed.

DeBoer's question: **why did so much passion produce so little policy?**

His answer is uncomfortable for the left: the movement was captured — from within — by the very elites it claimed to challenge.

---

## The Summer of 2020

The spark: the killing of George Floyd and its viral spread. The context: a pandemic that had already destabilised normal life, years of accumulated anger, and intersecting movements — Black Lives Matter, #MeToo, Bernie Sanders.

For a moment, the entire country seemed to speak in one voice. DeBoer's starting point: this was real. The anger was legitimate. The energy was genuine.

So what went wrong?

---

## Who Was Actually in the Streets?

DeBoer's uncomfortable empirical observation: the people doing the organising, messaging, protesting, and campaigning were **predominantly drawn from the educated, upwardly mobile classes** — not the working-class communities most affected by police violence, and not the economically marginalised people in whose name the movement spoke.

This matters — because the interests, instincts, and cultural priorities of educated elites are not the same as those of the people they claim to represent.

---

## Poses Over Policies

DeBoer's central diagnosis: the movement prioritised **symbolic action over material change.**

- Corporate diversity statements over wage increases
- Land acknowledgements over housing policy
- Social media callouts over legislative wins
- Renaming buildings over reforming institutions

This is not accidental. Symbolic action is cheap for elites and costs them nothing. Material change — redistribution, policy reform, structural transformation — threatens their interests.

**Calling out injustice is not the same as fighting it.**

---

## How Institutions Absorb Movements

DeBoer argues that established institutions have a remarkable capacity to absorb and neutralise radical energy. The process: movements generate energy and visibility, corporations and political parties rush to associate with the movement, they provide funding and legitimacy, and in exchange they gradually reshape the movement's goals toward what is acceptable to them. The radical edges are smoothed off. What remains is brand-compatible activism.

**The establishment doesn't defeat movements by opposing them. It defeats them by embracing them.**

---

## The Nonprofit Industrial Complex

One of deBoer's most detailed arguments: the role of the **nonprofit sector** in capturing social movements. Nonprofits depend on elite donors and foundations for funding, must produce measurable outputs to justify grants, tend toward professionalization and credentialism, and develop institutional interests in their own survival.

The result: activist energy is channelled into organisations that are structurally incapable of threatening the interests of the people who fund them.

---

## The Problem with Structurelessness

DeBoer also criticises the opposite tendency: movements that deliberately avoid structure, leadership, and organisation. The logic — hierarchy is oppressive, leadership creates accountability problems, decentralised networks are more resilient.

DeBoer's response: structurelessness does not eliminate power — it just makes power **invisible and unaccountable.** Without structure, the loudest, most connected, most privileged voices dominate by default.

---

## The Class Displacement Problem

DeBoer's deepest argument, rooted in his Marxist politics: when movements organise around **identity** rather than **class**, they systematically advantage the already advantaged within marginalised groups.

A Black Harvard graduate and a Black single mother in a depressed neighbourhood share a racial identity — but their material interests and political needs are very different. Identity-based movements tend to elevate the most articulate, credentialed, and connected members of marginalised groups.

**The people who most need change are the least likely to lead the movements that claim to speak for them.**

---

## What Actually Produces Change?

DeBoer looks at historical examples of successful movements — labour, civil rights, suffrage. Common features: clear, specific, legislative policy goals; broad coalition building across class lines; willingness to engage with electoral politics; organisational discipline and staying power; and persuasion of the unconvinced, not just mobilisation of the already convinced.

What the 2020 movement lacked: most of the above.

**Moral urgency is not a strategy. Outrage is not a programme.**

---

## The Messaging Problem

DeBoer argues the movement consistently failed at political communication: language developed within elite activist spaces that alienated ordinary voters, slogans like "Defund the Police" that were easily misrepresented and broadly unpopular, a tone of moral condemnation that closed off persuasion, and speaking to the already converted rather than the persuadable middle.

**You cannot shame people into agreeing with you.**

---

## DeBoer's Alternative Vision

DeBoer is not writing to comfort conservatives — he wants the left to win. His prescriptions:

- Organise around **class** as the primary category — it unites where identity divides
- Set **concrete, legislative policy goals** that can be measured and achieved
- Build **durable organisations** resistant to elite capture
- Develop **political language** that speaks to ordinary people
- Accept that **persuasion** — not moral purity — is the path to power
- Let affected communities **lead**, not credentialed outsiders

---

## Comparative Table

| | What the Movement Did | What DeBoer Says Works |
|--|----------------------|------------------------|
| **Focus** | Identity and symbolism | Class and material policy |
| **Tactics** | Protest and social media | Legislation and coalition building |
| **Organisation** | Structureless networks | Durable, accountable institutions |
| **Communication** | Insider activist language | Broad popular appeal |
| **Measure of success** | Moral visibility | Actual policy change |

---

## The Core Takeaway

The 2020 movement failed not because its grievances were illegitimate — they were real and urgent. It failed because it was captured by people whose class interests were served by symbolic change rather than structural transformation.

**The enemy of the left is not only the right. It is the comfortable, the credentialed, and the institutionalised — who absorb radical energy and return it as brand activism.**

Real change requires organisation, strategy, and the willingness to prioritise winning over being right.
