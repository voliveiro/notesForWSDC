# Keith Stanovich — *Myside Bias* (2021)
### Why Smart People Are Not Better Thinkers

---

## The Central Provocation

We assume that intelligence protects us from biased thinking. Smarter people reason better — right?

Stanovich's finding: **not when it comes to myside bias.**

Intelligent people are just as likely to reason in favour of their own prior beliefs. They are simply better at justifying what they already think.

---

## What Is Myside Bias?

Myside bias is the tendency to evaluate evidence in ways that favour your own prior beliefs, generate arguments that support your existing views, and fail to consider or fairly weigh opposing perspectives.

It is sometimes called **"my side" thinking** — reasoning like a lawyer defending a client, not a scientist testing a hypothesis. It is distinct from other cognitive biases — it is specifically about *whose side* the reasoning serves.

---

## The Shocking Finding

Most cognitive biases correlate with lower intelligence — smarter people show them less. Myside bias is different. It shows **virtually no correlation with IQ or cognitive ability.**

What higher intelligence *does* produce: more sophisticated, elaborate, and convincing justifications for pre-existing views. This is sometimes called the **sophistication effect** — intelligence in service of bias.

---

## The Lawyer vs The Scientist

Two models of reasoning:

| Lawyer | Scientist |
|--------|-----------|
| Starts with a conclusion | Starts with a question |
| Marshals evidence to support it | Follows evidence wherever it leads |
| Dismisses contrary evidence | Updates beliefs on new evidence |
| Wins by persuasion | Wins by being right |

Myside bias means most of us reason like lawyers most of the time — even when we think we're being objective.

---

## Why Intelligence Makes It Worse

A less intelligent person may hold a biased view but argue for it poorly. A highly intelligent person holds the same biased view and constructs more compelling arguments for it, identifies more weaknesses in opposing arguments, and is more persuasive to others — and to themselves.

Intelligence amplifies the *output* of myside bias without reducing its *presence*. **The smarter you are, the more dangerous your myside bias may be.**

---

## Myside Bias and Political Reasoning

Political beliefs are a particularly fertile ground for myside bias. Studies show that people across the political spectrum accept evidence that confirms their political views, dismiss or downgrade evidence that challenges them, and rate the same argument as stronger or weaker depending on its conclusion.

Partisan identity functions like a prior belief that shapes all subsequent reasoning. **Both sides do this equally — including the side that thinks it doesn't.**

---

## Convictions, Ideology, and the Two Reasons We Believe Things

Not all beliefs are equally resistant to revision. Stanovich draws a distinction that helps explain why ideological beliefs are particularly entrenched.

We hold beliefs for two fundamentally different kinds of reasons:

- **Epistemic reasons** — we believe something because the evidence seems to support it. The belief is, at least in principle, responsive to counter-evidence.
- **Utilitarian reasons** — we believe something because holding that belief is socially or psychologically useful. It signals group membership, confers status, and binds us to a community.

Many ideological convictions are held primarily for utilitarian reasons — not because we've independently evaluated the evidence, but because they are part of what it means to belong to our tribe. When that's the case, updating the belief isn't just an intellectual move. It's a social and identity cost: you risk losing standing, relationships, and a sense of who you are.

This is why **convictions make myside bias worse.** It isn't just that we have stronger priors — it's that the *price of being wrong* is much higher. Myside bias in service of a conviction isn't lazy reasoning; it's motivated defense of something that does real psychological and social work for you.

The practical implication is sobering: the domain where good reasoning matters most — politics, ethics, social policy — is precisely the domain where convictions are most ideologically loaded, and therefore where myside bias is most entrenched and most resistant to correction.

---

## The Frog in the Pot

One of Stanovich's key points: myside bias is largely invisible to the person experiencing it. We experience our own reasoning as objective and fair. We notice myside bias in others — rarely in ourselves.

This is what makes it so persistent and so difficult to correct. Unlike many biases, simply knowing about it does not reliably reduce it.

---

## What Does Reduce Myside Bias?

Stanovich's findings on debiasing:

- **Actively open-minded thinking (AOT)** — a disposition to seek out and fairly consider opposing views
- **Considering the opposite** — deliberately generating the strongest case against your position
- **Epistemic autonomy** — grounding beliefs in evidence rather than group identity

These are habits and dispositions — not just knowledge or intelligence. They can be cultivated, but they require effort and practice.

---

## Implications for Democratic Debate

If myside bias is universal and intelligence-independent, then public debate may produce more heat than light, expertise does not guarantee objectivity, education alone will not produce better reasoners, and institutional design matters — we need structures that force engagement with opposing views.

Democracy assumes citizens can reason together toward shared conclusions. **Myside bias puts that assumption under pressure.**

---

## The Core Takeaway

Intelligence is not a defence against motivated reasoning. The question is not how smart you are — it is **what your reasoning is in service of.**

Are you trying to find the truth, or trying to win?

The most important intellectual virtue is not intelligence. It is the **willingness to be wrong.**
