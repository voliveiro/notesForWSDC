# Jonathan Haidt — *The Righteous Mind* (2012)
### Why Good People are Divided by Politics and Religion

---

## The Central Provocation

We think moral disagreement is a reasoning problem. If only the other side thought more carefully, they would agree with us.

Haidt's challenge: **moral reasoning is not how we arrive at moral judgments.** It is how we justify them — after the fact.

The implication: better arguments alone will not resolve moral disagreement.

---

## The Rationalist Delusion

The dominant view in moral philosophy (Kant, Kohlberg): moral development means increasingly sophisticated reasoning, mature morality means universal principles arrived at by reason, and emotion is a distortion to be overcome.

Haidt's response: this describes almost no one's actual moral psychology. Real moral judgment is fast, automatic, and intuitive — reasoning comes later.

---

## The Emotional Dog and the Rational Tail

Haidt's central metaphor: the **dog** (intuition) wags, and the **tail** (reasoning) follows.

Moral intuitions arrive first — immediately, automatically, and with emotional force. Moral reasoning arrives second — and its primary function is **justification**, not truth-seeking.

We reason to explain and defend what we already feel — not to discover what we should feel.

---

## The Experiment: Moral Dumbfounding

Haidt tested this with scenarios designed to trigger moral intuition without obvious harm. Example: a consensual, private act between siblings that causes no lasting harm to either.

Most people immediately judge it as wrong. When pressed for reasons, they struggle — but they don't change their minds. They are **morally dumbfounded** — certain of their judgment, unable to justify it, unwilling to revise it.

Conclusion: the judgment came first. The reasoning was confabulation.

---

## Moral Foundations Theory

Human morality is built on several distinct psychological foundations — like **taste receptors.** Just as people have different taste preferences, they have different moral palettes.

| Foundation | Virtue | Vice |
|------------|--------|------|
| **Care** | Compassion | Cruelty |
| **Fairness** | Justice, reciprocity | Cheating |
| **Loyalty** | Group solidarity | Betrayal |
| **Authority** | Respect, hierarchy | Subversion |
| **Sanctity** | Purity, sacredness | Degradation |
| **Liberty** | Freedom, autonomy | Oppression |

---

## The Liberal-Conservative Divide

Haidt's empirical finding: liberals rely heavily on **Care** and **Fairness** — and are relatively unresponsive to the other foundations. Conservatives draw on **all six foundations** more evenly.

This means liberals often find conservative moral reasoning incomprehensible or disgusting, while conservatives often find liberal moral reasoning thin or naïve.

They are not reasoning badly — they are reasoning from **different moral foundations.**

---

## Morality Binds and Blinds

Haidt's double-edged claim about moral communities:

**Binds:** Shared moral foundations create group cohesion, trust, and cooperation. Without moral communities, collective action is impossible.

**Blinds:** The same foundations that bind a group make it harder to see outside it. Moral commitment to your group means motivated rejection of outgroup perspectives.

This is why moral communities are so good at sustaining themselves — and so bad at understanding each other.

---

## We Are All Intuitionists

Haidt is not saying moral reasoning is worthless. He is saying it is rarely the *cause* of moral judgment — it is usually the *product*.

Exceptions exist: slow, effortful reasoning can sometimes override intuition. But this is rare, costly, and usually only happens when we have no strong prior intuition, we are reasoning about distant or abstract cases, or we are genuinely motivated to get it right rather than to win.

---

## Connections to Stanovich

Both Haidt and Stanovich are diagnosing the same underlying problem from different angles:

| Stanovich | Haidt |
|-----------|-------|
| Myside bias — reasoning in favour of prior beliefs | Intuitions come first, reasoning justifies them |
| Uncorrelated with intelligence | Affects everyone regardless of education |
| Lawyer vs scientist model | Emotional dog vs rational tail |
| Debiasing requires AOT disposition | Debiasing requires understanding other foundations |

Together: **we are not the rational deliberators we think we are.**

---

## Implications for Democratic Debate

If moral disagreement is rooted in different foundational intuitions, then presenting better arguments may not persuade — it may just entrench, and dismissing the other side as stupid or evil misunderstands the psychology.

Effective persuasion requires speaking to the other side's moral foundations. Democratic deliberation needs to be redesigned around this reality.

**Haidt's practical suggestion:** moral humility — treating your own moral intuitions as data points, not certainties.

---

## The Core Takeaway

Moral disagreement is not primarily a failure of reasoning. It is a clash of different moral taste palettes — shaped by intuition, culture, and psychology.

Understanding this does not mean all moral views are equally valid. It means that **winning an argument is not the same as changing a mind** — and that moral persuasion requires empathy before it requires logic.
