# Michael Sandel — *The Tyranny of Merit* (2020)
### What's Become of the Common Good?

---

## The Central Provocation

We are told meritocracy is fair. If you work hard and develop your talents, you will succeed. If you fail, you have only yourself to blame.

Sandel's challenge: **meritocracy is not as fair as it seems — and even if it were, it would still be corrosive.**

The problem is not that we have failed to achieve meritocracy. The problem is what meritocracy does to us when it succeeds.

---

## What Is Meritocracy?

The term was coined — satirically — by Michael Young in 1958. Young imagined a dystopia where intelligence plus effort equals social position. He meant it as a warning. We took it as a blueprint.

**Core meritocratic promise:**
- Success reflects talent and hard work
- Social position is earned, not inherited
- The system is fair because everyone has a chance

---

## The Hubris of the Winner

When people believe they have earned their success through talent and effort, they tend to conclude: *I deserve what I have.* And by implication: *those who have less deserve less.*

Sandel calls this **meritocratic hubris** — an inflated sense of one's own desert that breeds contempt for those who fall behind. This is not a side effect of meritocracy. It is its logical conclusion.

---

## The Humiliation of the Loser

The flip side of meritocratic hubris is **meritocratic humiliation.**

In an aristocratic society, failure could be attributed to birth, luck, or an unjust system. In a meritocratic society, failure is your fault. You had the same chances as everyone else. You just weren't good enough.

This produces not just poverty — but shame. Sandel argues this humiliation is a major driver of the populist anger we see today.

---

## The Credential Obsession

Meritocracy in practice has become credential obsession. Elite universities function as sorting mechanisms for the professional class, producers of a self-reinforcing elite, and generators of enormous moral authority for their graduates.

The university degree has become a prerequisite for dignity in public life, a marker of who deserves to be listened to, and a source of class contempt directed downward.

**The credentialed look at the uncredentialed and see failure of effort or intelligence.**

---

## The Rhetoric of Rising

Politicians across the spectrum have embraced the same message: *"Work hard, play by the rules, and you can make it to the top."*

Sandel argues this rhetoric is empirically questionable (social mobility is limited), morally problematic (it implies those who don't rise deserve their fate), and politically corrosive (it dismisses the legitimate grievances of those left behind).

The rhetoric of rising tells the losers of globalisation: **your situation is your fault.**

---

## Do We Deserve Our Talents?

Sandel's deepest philosophical move: even if meritocracy were perfectly fair — equal opportunity, no inherited advantage — it would still be morally problematic.

Why? Because **we do not deserve our talents.** Natural abilities are unchosen, unearned, and distributed by luck. To reward talent as if it were a moral achievement is to reward luck.

---

## The Rawlsian Response — and Its Limits

Rawls made a similar argument: natural talents are "morally arbitrary." His solution: design institutions so that natural talent benefits everyone, especially the least advantaged.

Sandel agrees with the diagnosis but finds the solution insufficient. Rawls focuses on **distribution** — who gets what. Sandel argues we also need to focus on **recognition** — the social esteem and dignity accorded to different kinds of work and contribution.

Redistribution without recognition leaves the meritocratic culture of contempt intact.

---

## Reconsidering the Dignity of Work

Sandel argues that one of meritocracy's deepest failures is the **devaluation of non-credentialed work.** Market wages reflect market power — not social contribution or moral worth.

A hedge fund manager earns more than a nurse. A tech entrepreneur earns more than a sanitation worker. Meritocracy tells us this reflects desert. Sandel says: it reflects the accident of what the market happens to reward.

We need a renewed public debate about the **social value** of different contributions.

---

## The Common Good

Sandel's alternative to meritocracy is not equality of outcome — it is a renewed sense of the **common good.** This requires:

- Civic spaces where people of different backgrounds mix and deliberate
- Public institutions that are genuinely shared — not tiered by credential
- A moral culture that esteems contribution, not just achievement
- Political rhetoric that speaks to citizens as contributors, not as consumers or human capital

The goal is not to eliminate excellence — it is to stop treating excellence as the only thing that matters.

---

## Comparative Table

| | Meritocracy | Sandel's Alternative |
|--|-------------|----------------------|
| **Basis of reward** | Talent + effort | Contribution to common good |
| **Source of dignity** | Individual achievement | Civic recognition |
| **View of success** | Deserved | Partly luck, partly circumstance |
| **Political rhetoric** | Rhetoric of rising | Rhetoric of shared citizenship |
| **Risk** | Hubris and contempt | Requires robust public culture |

---

## The Core Takeaway

The problem with meritocracy is not that it is unfair. The problem is what it does to us — winners and losers alike. It breeds hubris at the top, humiliation at the bottom, and contempt across the middle. It mistakes the accident of talent for moral desert. And it corrodes the sense of shared fate that democratic citizenship requires.

**The question is not how to make meritocracy work better. It is what we want to value — and why.**
