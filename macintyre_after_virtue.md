# Alasdair MacIntyre — *After Virtue* (1981)
### A Moral Critique of Liberal Modernity

---

## The Central Thesis

> **We live after the collapse of a coherent moral tradition.**

Modern moral language retains fragments of older concepts — virtue, justice, rights — but has lost the framework that gave them meaning.

**Result:** Moral debate becomes interminable and shrill.

---

## The "Disaster" Hypothesis

MacIntyre opens with a thought experiment: imagine science collapses. People preserve fragments of scientific vocabulary but lose the underlying theory. That is our condition with morality.

We use terms like justice, rights, equality, and autonomy — but without a shared framework, they float free.

---

## The Failure of the Enlightenment Project

The Enlightenment attempted to ground morality on reason alone, universal principles, and individual autonomy — think Kant and Hume.

**MacIntyre's claim:** This project failed. Why? Because it tried to justify moral norms after discarding **teleology** (purpose).

---

## Teleology: The Missing Piece

In classical thought (e.g. Aristotle):
- Humans have a *telos* — an end or purpose
- Virtue is what enables flourishing
- Moral rules serve human goods

Modern liberal thought rejects telos. Without a shared conception of the human good, moral rules become arbitrary.

---

## Emotivism

MacIntyre diagnoses modern moral culture as **emotivist** — meaning moral judgments merely express preferences. "X is wrong" means "I disapprove of X."

If this is true:
- Moral debate becomes a power struggle
- Persuasion becomes manipulation

Liberal public culture implicitly assumes emotivism.

---

## Liberalism and the Sovereign Self

Modern liberal society assumes the individual chooses their own ends, society must remain neutral between conceptions of the good, and the self is prior to its roles and traditions.

MacIntyre argues this produces the **"unencumbered self"** — a self detached from history, community, and shared narrative.

---

## The Fragmentation of Moral Discourse

Modern debates about justice, rights, and equality use incommensurable premises and have no shared standard of rational adjudication — they become endless and polarised.

This explains why liberal societies experience perpetual moral disagreement and procedural rather than substantive consensus.

---

## The Manager and the Therapist

MacIntyre identifies two emblematic modern character types:

- **The Manager** — technocratic, claims value-neutral expertise
- **The Therapist** — prioritises personal satisfaction and authenticity

These embody liberal modernity: efficiency replaces virtue, preference replaces the good.

---

## Practices and Internal Goods

MacIntyre proposes an alternative framework. A **practice** is a socially established cooperative activity with standards of excellence that pursues internal goods — examples include medicine, teaching, craftsmanship, and politics (ideally).

Virtues are cultivated within practices.

---

## Tradition as Rational Context

Contrary to liberal suspicion of tradition, MacIntyre argues traditions are historically extended arguments, carriers of rationality, and frameworks within which goods are defined.

**Rationality is tradition-dependent. There is no neutral standpoint.**

---

## Critique of Liberal Neutrality

Liberalism claims the state should be neutral between conceptions of the good. MacIntyre responds: neutrality is impossible. Every political order privileges certain goods, shapes certain character types, and encourages certain virtues — or vices.

**Liberalism forms consumers and managers.**

---

## Virtue vs Preference

| Modern Liberal Order | Classical Virtue Ethics |
|----------------------|------------------------|
| Elevates choice | Forms character |
| Protects preference | Orders desires |
| Encourages self-definition | Directs the self toward flourishing |

The difference is anthropological, not merely political.

---

## Why Liberal Modernity Feels Disordered

Because we retain moral language, but reject shared teleology, and deny formative community.

**The result:** Moral fragmentation, bureaucratic management, and loss of common goods.

---

## MacIntyre's Closing Image

The final line of *After Virtue* invokes **"another — doubtless very different — St. Benedict."**

Meaning: withdrawal into local communities, preservation of virtue traditions, and moral formation at smaller scales.

Not revolution. **Reconstruction.**

---

## How This Critiques Liberalism

MacIntyre's challenge to liberal ideas:
- Autonomy without telos collapses into emotivism
- Neutrality dissolves shared moral reasoning
- Procedural justice cannot substitute for shared goods
- Managerial expertise replaces virtue

**The crisis is not political first. It is moral and anthropological.**
